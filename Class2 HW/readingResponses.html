Before taking this class, I haven’t really thought of the feminist and political elements that hide behind the construction of the internet and website. This week’s reading about cyberfeminism is very informative and thought-provoking. After reading the cyberfeminist manifesto, there were too many thoughts in my head, which make me don’t know where to get started from. Week 1 Response


When reading J.R Carpenter’s A Handmade Web, I found it interesting that the handmade web is closely linked to the creator and a person’s identity and how people view handmade web and public big sites differently. They host the creator’s identity in personal ways and are one of a kind. The handmade web has more intimate and quieter. The emotions contained in a handmade web seem to be more obvious and the connections between people and handmade we are stronger. Reading this article makes me realize a phenomenon that not only happens in the internet world but exists in the entire human development history. People always tend to start from something small and intimate to something big, popular, and crowded, whereas, somehow, in the end, people would always find a way to slow down and go back to the initial idea with something quieter and smaller.

In Olia Lillian’s “Vernacular Web”, the concept and statement about translucency, transparency, and how developers and users perceive the web provides me with a new way of thinking about the current usage and functionality of the web. I believe I am one of the amateurs and users that almost know nothing about how a web is crafted or functions as Olia mentioned in the article. I used to think that an efficient and understandable interface is a good and necessary quality, but now I am not sure if this is still a good thing for the user or if it imperceptibly enlarges the gap between the user and understanding the interface and the mindset between designers and users. Besides, It is quite interesting to learn that web2.0 has more attraction or features but still follows some rules and traditions from web1.0 and non-digital mediums.

When reading Laurel Schwulst’s article, I really like and agree with how Laurel Schwulst describes the website as “they’re both subject and object at once. In other words, a website creator becomes both author and architect simultaneously.” I completely agree with Laurel Schwulst’s opinion that the “web needs to actually work for people instead of being powered by a small handful of big corporations.” It makes me start thinking of the power dynamics within the web, and the relationship between developers and users. I am inspired by how Laurel Schwulst uses metaphors to demonstrate how flexible a website can be and what world people can create with its possibilities and potential for each individual.
